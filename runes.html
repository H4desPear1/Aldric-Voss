The Codex Noctis

Manuscript documentation, transcription, and research notes — Voss Runic Studies Project, 2019–2024
Project Overview
Codex Noctis Study · Lead Investigator: Dr. Aldric Voss · Status: Ongoing at time of death

The Codex Noctis (provisional title, assigned by Voss, 2019) is an anonymous manuscript of disputed provenance, believed to date to the 9th–11th century CE based on preliminary material analysis. The codex consists of a cover illustration and three pages of dense runic inscription using a script system that combines recognizable Elder Futhark characters with a series of glyphs whose phonological values remain contested.

The manuscript came to Ashford University through an estate donation in 2018 and was assigned to Dr. Voss for formal study in early 2019. His research aimed to produce the first complete transcription and contextual analysis of the text. At the time of his death, all three inscription pages had been transcribed but only partially deciphered.

This page presents the manuscript documentation in full, including the cover image and all three pages of runic text, along with Dr. Voss's working research notes.

Cover Illustration
Folio 1r — Pigment on vellum · Undated · High-resolution scan

The codex opens with a full-page illustration executed in mineral pigments on stretched vellum. The image depicts a complex symbolic composition that Voss described in his notes as a "cosmological diagram of unusual density." It appears to serve as a key or orientation device for the inscriptions that follow, though the precise relationship between image and text has not been established.

Notable features include a central circular motif enclosing a tripartite division, flanked by two upright figural forms. The border contains repeating decorative elements that, on close inspection, incorporate isolated runic characters not found in the main inscription pages. Voss's note in the margin of his working copy reads: "The image is not decorative. It is instructional."

Page 1 — First Inscription Partially Deciphered
Folio 2r · 14 lines · Elder Futhark base with substitution cipher elements

The first inscription page presents fourteen lines of runic text in a regular horizontal layout. Voss identified this page as the most structurally conventional of the three, with the highest proportion of recognizable Elder Futhark characters. His working hypothesis was that this page functions as an introduction or invocation and may contain proper names or place references.

Page 2 — Second Inscription Undeciphered
Folio 2v · 12 lines + marginal annotations · Mixed cipher system

The second inscription page is the most complex of the three. It introduces a cipher layer that Voss could not reconcile with any known historical system. A series of small marginal annotations in a different hand suggest the codex may have had more than one author, or that it was used and amended by subsequent readers. Voss was particularly troubled by the marginal marks, which appear to function as correction notations.

Page 3 — Third Inscription Transcribed
Folio 3r · 10 lines · Dense cipher layer · Final page of codex

The third page was the last Dr. Voss worked on. His notes indicate he considered it the most urgent, describing it in correspondence as "the page that changes everything." It is the most heavily ciphered of the three inscriptions, and Voss's transcription notes contain more uncertainty markers than the others. The final two lines appear to have been added in a different ink, suggesting a later amendment.
